\section{The rise and fall of ordinary Quantum Mechanics} 
%=========================================================
%----------------------------------------------------------


\begin{mytheorem}[The rise of ordinary Quantum Mechanics.]
At the turn of the 20th century, several experimental observations challenged the classical understanding of physics and led to the development of Quantum Mechanics.
Some of the key discoveries are the following.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Blackbody Radiation.} Max Planck's solution to the ultraviolet catastrophe by introducing the concept of quantized energy levels.
    \item \textbf{Photoelectric Effect.} Albert Einstein's explanation of the photoelectric effect, proposing that light consists of discrete packets of energy called photons.
    \item \textbf{Atomic Spectra \& stability of matter.} Niels Bohr's model of the hydrogen atom, introducing quantized orbits for electrons, solved the problem of radiation by accelerated charges that would predict electrons to spiral down into the nucleus.
    Ultimately, the solution was found in the uncertainty principle: as $\Delta x \Delta p \gtrsim \hbar$, as the electron gets closer to the nucleus, its momentum uncertainty increases, preventing it from collapsing into the nucleus.
    \item \textbf{Wave-Particle Duality \& double-slit experiment.} Louis de Broglie's hypothesis that particles exhibit wave-like properties, leading to the development of wave mechanics by Schrödinger.
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty Principle.} Heisenberg's formulation of the uncertainty principle, which states that certain pairs of physical properties cannot be simultaneously known to arbitrary precision.
\end{itemize}
\end{mytheorem}


\begin{mytheorem}[The price of noncommutativity.]
If we demand that observables are represented by noncommuting operators, we have to pay a price: such observable must be operators acting on some vector space $\mathcal{H}$, i.e. they must be matrices, since standard numbers commute.
Furthermore, taking the trace of the canonical commutation relations gives
\begin{align}
    [\hat{q}_i, \hat{p}_j] = i\hbar\delta_{ij} \Longrightarrow 0=\text{Tr}([\hat{q}_i, \hat{p}_j]) = i\hbar \,\text{Tr}(\delta_{ij}) = i \hbar \cdot \dim(\mathcal{H}).
\end{align}
The only way to resolve this absurdity is to have $\dim(\mathcal{H}) = \infty$, so that the trace is not well-defined and the above equality is meaningless.
The take home is that such operators must act on an \emph{infinite}-dimensional Hilbert space.
Note the problem would get even worse if we had a continuum of DoFs, since then we would have to deal with $\text{Tr}\,\delta(x-y)$ in the above equality, which is even more ill-defined and infinite.

Note that this is not necessarily the case for all quantum systems: for instance, spin systems have a finite number of DoFs and can be described by finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces.
The primary example is the spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ system, whose Hilbert space is just $\mathbb{C}^2$ with Pauli matrices as operators obeying $[\sigma_i, \sigma_j] = 2i \epsilon_{ijk} \sigma_k$.
\end{mytheorem}


\begin{mytheorem}[The fall of ordinary Quantum Mechanics.]
Despite its successes, Quantum Mechanics soon after faced several challenges progressively highlighting its limitations, up to making it untenable, and paving the way for the development of Quantum Field Theory.
%
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Incompatibility with special relativity \& negative energies.} Quantum Mechanics, as originally formulated, was not compatible with the principles of special relativity.
    Attempts to create a relativistic version of Quantum Mechanics, such as the Klein-Gordon and Dirac equations, led to difficulties in interpreting negative energy solutions and the need for a new framework.
    \item \textbf{Particle Creation and Annihilation.} Quantum Mechanics treated particles as fixed entities, unable to account for processes where particles are created or destroyed, which are common in high-energy physics.
    This limitation necessitated a theory that could handle variable particle numbers.
    \item \textbf{Infinities and Renormalization.} Early attempts to apply Quantum Mechanics to electromagnetic interactions resulted in infinities in calculated quantities, such as the electron's self-energy.
    The development of renormalization techniques was required to make sense of these infinities, but it indicated that a more fundamental theory was needed.
    \item \textbf{Gauge Invariance and Interactions.} Quantum Mechanics struggled to incorporate gauge invariance, a fundamental symmetry underlying the interactions of fundamental forces.
    The need for a framework that could naturally include gauge fields and their interactions led to the development of Quantum Field Theory.
\end{itemize}
\end{mytheorem}


\begin{mytheorem}[A first (failed) attempt: Relativistic Quantum Mechanics.]
%
A first attempt to address the limitations of Quantum Mechanics aimed to combine the principles of quantum mechanics with special relativity, summarized in the following diagram:

The idea is simply to promote the Schr\"odinger equation to a relativistic equation, such as the Klein-Gordon or Dirac equations, without enforcing the second quantization procedure i.e. the wave function is still a c-number and not an operator, connected with the probability amplitude of finding a particle at a given position and time.
This approach successfully incorporated relativistic effects and predicted phenomena such as antimatter, but still faced significant problems, including the interpretation of negative energy solutions and the inability to account for particle creation and annihilation processes.

For example, both the Schr\"odinger and Klein-Gordon equations imply a continuity equation {\small $\partial_t\rho=-\nabla\cdot \mathbf{j}$}, identifying a probability density $\rho$ and current $\mathbf{j}$, namely
\begin{align}
    &i\hbar \partial_t \psi = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \psi \implies
    \partial_t|\psi|^2 = -\nabla\cdot \Big(\tfrac{i\hbar}{2m}(\psi \nabla \psi^*- \psi^* \nabla \psi)\Big),
    \\[3pt]
    &(\Box+m^2)\phi = 0 \implies \partial_t\Big(i(\phi^* \partial_t \phi - \phi \partial_t \phi^*)\Big) = -\nabla\cdot \Big(i(\phi \nabla \phi^* - \phi \nabla \phi)\Big).
\end{align}
In the Schr\"odinger case, we find the familiar positive definite probability density $\rho = |\psi|^2$.
In the Klein-Gordon case, recalling solutions take the form {\small $\phi=N e^{-iEt+i\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{x}}$} with {\small $E^2 = |\mathbf{k}|^2 + m^2$}, we find $\rho = 2E|N|^2$, which is not positive definite since $E$ can be negative.

Crucially, the problem of negative energies is not due to the equation being second-order in time derivatives, which inevitably involves square roots.
In fact, Dirac tried to solve the problem by seeking an equation that was first-order in time derivatives, and thus also in space derivatives to treat space and time on an equal footing.
The Dirac equation still implies a continuity equation, namely
\begin{align}
    (i\gamma^\mu \partial_\mu - m)\psi = 0\implies \partial_t(\psi^\dagger \psi) = -\nabla_j\cdot (\psi^\dagger \gamma^0 \gamma^j \psi).
\end{align}
The Dirac equation does avoid negative probability density $\rho = \psi^\dagger \psi\geq0$ in the continuity equation, but still predicts negative energy solutions.
Indeed, the two solutions are positive $u(k)e^{-iEt+i\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{x}}$ and negative-energy $v(k)e^{iEt+i\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{x}}$ spinors with $E^2 = |\mathbf{k}|^2 + m^2$, which he interpreted as antiparticles.

Regardless of negative energies, the interpretation of the wave function as the probability amplitude for finding a single particle at a given space-time position is still problematic, since it cannot account for processes where particles are created or destroyed, very common in high-energy physics.
In fact, even for energies lower than the rest mass energy of target particles, because of the uncertainty principle, a consistent relativistic quantum theory must allow for the creation and annihilation of virtual particle-antiparticle pairs.

The interpretation of the wave function as the probability amplitude for a single particle is also problematic because it violates causality.
Indeed, for a free hamiltonian $H = \sqrt{p^2 + m^2}$, the propagator $\langle \mathbf{x} | e^{-iHt} | \mathbf{y} \rangle$ is non-zero also for spacelike separations $|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}| > t$, thus allowing for superluminal propagation of signals.
Specifically we find an exponentially suppressed but non-vanishing amplitude 
\begin{align}\label{eq:nonvanishing_superluminal_propagation_apmplitude}
\begin{aligned}
    \langle \mathbf{x} | e^{-iHt} | \mathbf{y} \rangle &= \int \frac{d^3p}{(2\pi)^3} e^{i\mathbf{p}\cdot(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}) - i\sqrt{|\mathbf{p}|^2 + m^2}t} \\
    &\sim e^{-m\sqrt{|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}|^2 - t^2}} \quad \text{for } |\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}| \gg t.
\end{aligned}\end{align}

The above issues highlighted the need for a more comprehensive framework, ultimately leading to the development of Quantum Field Theory, treating fields as the fundamental entities and allowing for the creation and annihilation of particles, which are just reinterpreted as excitations of the underlying fields.
We can already anticipate that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the problem of negative energies is resolved since the mathematical epsression for the energy will be different than the simple $i\hbar\partial_t$ operator;
    \item the problem of particle creation and annihilation is resolved since the wave function will be promoted to an operator, thus allowing for a variable number of particles;
    \item the problem of causality is resolved since there is no point in asking whether "the field is at a position $\mathbf{x}$ at a given time $t$", since the field is `everywhere' at all times, and thus the question is simply ill-posed.
    In fact, the formula analogous to \eqref{eq:nonvanishing_superluminal_propagation_apmplitude} for the field propagator in QFT is still non-zero for spacelike separations, albeit exponentially suppressed.

    The proper question is instead whether "the state of the field at a given spacetime point can influence the state of the field at another spacetime point", i.e. a question about causal correlators, which will be shown to vanish for spacelike separations.
    We also anticipate that the role of antiparticles is crucial for this causl correlator to vanish for spacelike separations, since the contribution of antiparticles exactly cancels the contribution of particles in this case.
\end{itemize}
\end{mytheorem}




\begin{mytheorem}[How to make the Schr\"odinger equation relativistic? Klein-Gordon is \emph{always} the answer.]
%
Regardless of first or second quantization, i.e. both for Relativistic Quantum Mechanics and Quantum Field Theory, a necessary step towards a relativistic quantum theory is to formulate a relativistic version of the Schr\"odinger equation.
The Schr\"odinger equation is first-order in time and second-order in space derivatives, and enforces a non-relativistic energy-momentum relation
{\small\begin{equation}
    -i\partial_t\psi = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \psi\quad \Longrightarrow\quad E = \frac{p^2}{2m}.
\end{equation}}
First of all, in order to be relativistic, the equation must treat space and time on an equal footing.
Several approaches were attempted, ultimately depending on the spin of the particle considered.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Klein-Gordon Equation.} For spin-0 particles, the Klein-Gordon equation was proposed:
    \begin{equation}
        (\partial_{\mu} \partial^{\mu} + m^2) \phi(x) = 0\,.
    \end{equation}
    This is second-order in both time and space derivatives and respects the relativistic energy-momentum relation \(E^2 = p^2 + m^2\).
    However, it faced challenges in interpreting negative energy solutions and the associated probability density.
    \item \textbf{Dirac Equation.} For spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ particles, Dirac proposed a `square root' of the Klein-Gordon equation that would be first-order in both time and space derivatives
    \begin{equation}
        (i\gamma^{\mu} \partial_{\mu} - m) \psi(x) = 0.
    \end{equation}
    The introduction of $\gamma$-matrices was necessary to provide another Lorentz vector to contract with $\partial_{\mu}$ to make the equation Lorentz invariant.
    More physically, Dirac proposed that the energy, i.e. the Hamiltonian, could be expressed as a linear combination of momentum and mass
    \begin{align}
        H= a_i p_i + b m .
    \end{align}
    Enforcing the relativistic energy-momentum relation $E^2 = p^2 + m^2$ leads to
    \begin{align}
        E^2 = \tfrac{a_ia_j+a_ja_i}{2}p_ip_j+ (a_ib+ba_i) p_i + b^2 m^2 \implies
        \{a_i, a_j\} = 2\delta_{ij}, \quad a_i b + b a_i = 0, \quad b^2 = 1.
    \end{align}
    Passing to the usual representation of energy $E = i\partial_t$ and momentum $p_i = -i\partial_i$ eventually leads to the Dirac equation above.
    The Dirac equation can indeed be derived by seeking a first-order equation whose square reproduces the Klein-Gordon equation:
    \begin{align}
        ia^\mu\partial_\mu\psi + b \psi = 0 &\Longrightarrow (ia^\mu\partial_\mu + b)^*(ia^\nu\partial_\nu + b)\psi = 0 \\
        &\Longrightarrow \big[a^\mu a^\nu \partial_\mu \partial_\nu + i(b a^\mu-a^\mu b)\partial_\mu + b^2\big]\psi = 0.
    \end{align}
    Imposing this matches the Klein-Gordon equation requires
    \begin{align}
        \{a^\mu, a^\nu\} = 2\eta^{\mu\nu}, \quad
        a^\mu b - b a^\mu = 0, \quad
        b^2= m^2\quad \Rightarrow \quad a^\mu\equiv \gamma^\mu, \quad b \equiv m\,.
    \end{align}
    These relations cannot be satisfied by ordinary numbers, leading to the introduction of $\gamma$-matrices.
    The Dirac equation successfully incorporated spin and predicted the existence of antimatter, but similarly introduced complexities related to negative energy states.
    %
    \item For spin-1 particles, the Proca and Maxwell equation was developed--or better borrowed from classical electrodynamics--to describe massive and massless vector bosons respectively:
    \begin{equation}
        \partial_{\mu} F^{\mu\nu} + m^2 A^{\nu} = 0.
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
All these equations ultimately obey the Klein-Gordon equation, as it should be since it encodes the relativistic energy-momentum relation \(E^2 = p^2 + m^2\) that all particles must satisfy.
\end{mytheorem}


\begin{mytheorem}[The idea of second quantization: from relativistic QM to Quantum Field Theory]
At some point people started wondering: in quantum mechanics everything is subject to fluctuations, why not the very number of particles themselves? 
In a sense this amounts to allowing the wave function itself fluctuate, oscillating between the wave function of a single particle, two particles, three particles, and so on.
More rigorously, why not to promote the wave function to an operator itself? 

The name of \emph{second quantization} alludes to this idea of quantizing once again the wave function, which was already a quantum object.
Beware this is very misleading: quantization is performed only once and the ordinary QM wave function is not replaced by the field operator.
The pointis that we are now tracking an infinite number of DoFs, namely a field, and just as we quantized the position and momentum operator in the case of a finite number of DoFs, we now quantize the field operator and its conjugate momentum.
The wave function of the theory still exists in the usual sense, and is not replaced by the field operator, which instead is simply the infinite-dimensional analogoue of the $q_j$ and $p_j$ operators.
\end{mytheorem}

\begin{mytheorem}[What is a quantum (field) theory?]
Ultimately, when developing any quantum theory to model a system, we only have 3 ingredients.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The DoFs.} We need to identify the relevant DoFs of the system.
    In particular, a quantum \emph{field} theory is just a theory where the fundamental DoFs are fields $\phi(x)$.
    %
    \item \textbf{The symmetries.} We need to identify the symmetries of the system, which are typically encoded in the transformation properties of the DoFs under certain symmetry groups.
    Notable examples are invariance under the Galilean or Lorentz group, for non-relativistic and relativistic theories respectively, and gauge invariance or other internal symmetries for theories describing fundamental interactions.
    %
    \item \textbf{The dynamics.} We need to specify the dynamics of the system, which is typically done by writing down a Lagrangian or Hamiltonian for DoFs, consistent with the symmetries of the system.
\end{itemize}
In practice one often identifies the possible DoFs by classifying the representations of the symmetry group of interest and then writing down (the most general) lagrangian consistent with these symmetries.
The fundamental constituents of the theory will correspond to irreducible representations of the symmetry group, while composite objects will correspond to reducible representations.

The symmetry group will typically be the direct product of the spacetime symmetry group and the internal symmetry group, which are often independent.
For nonrelativistic theories, the relevant spacetime symmetry group is the Galilean group, while for relativistic theories is the Poincaré group: the semi-direct product of the Lorentz group and the group of spacetime translations.
\end{mytheorem}



\begin{mytheorem}[Heisenberg canonical quantization procedure.]
%
The Heisenberg canonical quantization procedure is a systematic method for constructing a quantum theory from a classical theory, namely:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Promote the phase-space variables \(q_i, p_i\), i.e. the classical DoFs of the theory to operators acting on a Hilbert space;
    %
    \item Poisson brackets are replaced by commutators according to the rule {\small$\displaystyle \{A, B\} \mapsto \frac{1}{i\hbar} [\hat{A}, \hat{B}];$}
    %
    \item the equations of motion are left formally unchanged, but now refer to evolving operators.
\end{itemize}
In practice, we start from the classical phase-space variables \(q_i, p_i\) satisfying the relevant Equations of Motions and  Poisson brackets, possibly in the continuum limit {\small$\delta_{ij} \mapsto \delta(x-y)$} as the number of DoFs goes to infinity,
{\small\begin{align}
\{q_i, p_j\} = \delta_{ij}, \quad \{q_i, q_j\} = 0, \quad \{p_i, p_j\} = 0.
\end{align}}
We promote these variables to \emph{self-adjoint} operators \(\hat{q}_i, \hat{p}_i\) acting on a Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\), and replace the Poisson brackets with the canonical commutation relations, again possibly in the continuum limit,
{\small\begin{align}
[\hat{q}_i, \hat{p}_j] = i\hbar \delta_{ij}, \quad [\hat{q}_i, \hat{q}_j] = 0, \quad [\hat{p}_i, \hat{p}_j] = 0.
\end{align}}
These operators satisfy the \emph{same equations of motion as the classical counterparts}, but now in the operator form
{\small\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\hat{O}}{dt} = \frac{1}{i\hbar} [\hat{O},\hat{H}] + \Big(\frac{\partial \hat{O}}{\partial t}\Big)_{\text{explicit}}.
\end{equation}}
In particular, for the fundamental DoFs we have
{\small\begin{align}
    i\hbar\frac{d\hat{q}_i}{dt} = [\hat{q}_i, \hat{H}] \quad i\hbar\frac{d\hat{p}_i}{dt} = [\hat{p}_i, \hat{H}].
\end{align}}
\end{mytheorem}



\begin{mytheorem}[Heisenberg vs Schr\"odinger picture \& interaction picture]
There are 2+1 ways to describe the time evolution of a system in Quantum Mechanics.
These are completely equivalent and related by a unitary transformation.
\begin{itemize}
    \item[]\textbf{Heisenberg picture}. Operators evolve in time according to the Heisenberg equation of motion, while state vectors remain constant.
    \item[]\textbf{Schr\"odinger picture}. State vectors evolve in time according to the Schr\"odinger equation, while operators remain constant.
    \item[]\textbf{Interaction picture}. Both operators and state vectors evolve in time, accoring to a splitting of the Hamiltonian $\hat{H} = \hat{H}_0 + \hat{H}_{\text{int}}$ into a free part and an interaction part.
\end{itemize}
In the Schr\"odinger picture states evolves in time as
{\small\begin{equation}
    i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H} |\psi(t)\rangle.
\end{equation}}
The Schr\"odinger picture provides more intuition from classical physics and is often more convenient for practical calculations, especially in time-dependent problems.

On the other hand, the Heisenberg picture is particularly useful in Quantum Field Theory, and in the study of symmetries and conservation laws.
Moreover, it makes the connection with classical mechanics transparent via the canonical quantization procedure: the EoM are left unchanged, classical DoFs are promoted to operators, and Poisson brackets become commutators.
In particular, it makes it easier to see how classical observables correspond to quantum operators and to recover the classical limit of quantum systems when $\hbar \to 0$.

Finally, the interaction picture is used in perturbation theory and in the study of time-dependent interactions, as it allows for a separation of the free evolution from the effects of interactions.
\end{mytheorem}


\begin{mytheorem}[Ehrenfest theorem.]
The Ehrenfest theorem establishes a connection between the quantum mechanical expectation values of observables and their classical counterparts.
It states that the time evolution of the expectation value
of an operator \(\hat O\) in a quantum state \(|\psi(t)\rangle\) is
{\small\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt} \langle \hat{O} \rangle = \frac{1}{i\hbar} \langle [\hat{O}, \hat{H}] \rangle + \Big\langle \Big(\frac{\partial \hat{O}}{\partial t}\Big)_{\text{explicit}} \Big\rangle,
\end{equation}}
where the expectation value  is \(\langle \hat{O} \rangle = \langle \psi(t) | \hat{O} | \psi(t) \rangle\) and \(\hat{H}\) is the Hamiltonian operator.

For a quadratic Hamiltonian, the time evolution of the expectation value is then exactly the same as the classical evolution of the corresponding observable \(O\).
For non-quadratic Hamiltonians, classical motion is reproduced only approximately, for example when the wave packet is sufficiently localized.

For example, for \(\hat H=\hat p^{\,2}/(2m)+V(\hat q)\), applying the Ehrenfest theorem to the position and momentum operators gives
{\small\begin{align}
    \frac{d}{dt}\langle\hat q_i\rangle
    &=
    \frac{1}{m}\langle\hat p_i\rangle,
    &
    \frac{d}{dt}\langle\hat p_i\rangle
    &=
    -\left\langle\frac{\partial V}{\partial q_i}\right\rangle.
\end{align}}
These equations reproduce the corresponding classical versions exactly
for free particles and, more generally, for potentials at most
quadratic.
For non-quadratic potentials classical motion is only aproximately reproduced under suitable conditions, e.g. if the wave packet is sufficiently
localized that
{\small\begin{align}
    \left\langle\frac{\partial V}{\partial q_i}\right\rangle
    \simeq
    \left.
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial q_i}
    \right|_{q=\langle\hat q\rangle}.
\end{align}}
The Ehrenfest theorem thus provides a bridge between quantum and classical mechanics, showing that the average behavior of quantum systems approximately follows classical laws under certain conditions.
\end{mytheorem}



\begin{mytheorem}[Stone-von Neumann theorem.]
%
By the postulates, the Hilbert space of quantum mechanics are required to be separable.
In particular, any two spaces with the same number of DoFs, finite or countable infinite, are always unitarily isomorphic
{\small\begin{align}
    U:\mathcal{H}_1 \to \mathcal{H}_2, \quad U^\dagger U = \mathbb{I}_{\mathcal{H}_1}, \quad U U^\dagger = \mathbb{I}_{\mathcal{H}_2}.
\end{align}}  
The Stone-von Neumann theorem states that, if the number of degrees of freedom is finite, there is a unique (up to unitary isomorphisms) irreducible representation of the canonical commutation relations (CCR) for the DoFs of the system.
That is, denoting the DoFs of the system as $\{q_i, p_i\}_{i=1}^N$, any two irreducible representations $a=1,2$ of the CCR 
{\small\begin{align}
    [\hat{q}^a_i, \hat{p}^a_j] = i\hbar \delta_{ij}, \quad [\hat{q}^a_i, \hat{q}^a_j] = 0, \quad [\hat{p}^a_i, \hat{p}^a_j] = 0,
\end{align}}
on separable Hilbert spaces $\mathcal{H}_a$ are unitarily isomorphic in the sense that the unitary operator \(U: \mathcal{H}_1 \to \mathcal{H}_2\) can be taken so that
{\small\begin{align}
    U \hat{q}^{(1)}_i U^\dagger = \hat{q}^{(2)}_i, \quad U \hat{p}^{(1)}_i U^\dagger = \hat{p}^{(2)}_i.
\end{align}}
This theorem ensures that the physical predictions of Quantum Mechanics are independent of the specific representation chosen for the operators, as long as the number of degrees of freedom is finite.

The theorem \emph{does not hold} when the number of degrees of freedom is infinite, as in Quantum Field Theory.
In typical cases, we can impose IR regularization, making the DoFs countable discrete, and UV regularization, making the DoFs finite, and thus recover the theorem.
However, this regularization procedure might fail in particular circumstances, leading to unitarily \emph{inequivalent} representations of the CCR.
For example:
\begin{itemize}
    \item if a (weird) theory does not enjoy a proper separation of scales, so that low energy physics is sensitive to the UV completion and the physical predictions depend on the UV regularization scheme;
    \item in the context of phase transitions, where different phases of the system can be described by unitarily inequivalent representations of the CCR, reflecting the distinct physical properties of each phase. Indeed, by the Lee-Yang theorem, phase transitions can only occur in the thermodynamic limit, where the space is \emph{truly} infinite and the number of DoFs must then be \emph{uncountable} infinite (cf. the Fourier modes of $L^2([0,1]^d)$ versus $L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)$).
\end{itemize}
%
\end{mytheorem}

\begin{mytheorem}[Representation of Lorentz group on fields]
As discussed in Section \ref{sec:guiding_theorems}, a quantum theory ultimately boils down to 3 ingredients: the DoFs, the symmetries, and the dynamics.
In practice one often identifies the possible DoFs by classifying the representations of the symmetry group of interest and then writing down (the most general) lagrangian consistent with these symmetries.

For relativistic theories, the relevant symmetry group is the Poincaré group: the semi-direct product of the Lorentz group and the group of spacetime translations.
Since the Lorentz group is non-compact, its unitary representations must be infinite-dimensional.

Indeed finite-dimensional representation of the Lorentz algebra are classified by two half-integer numbers $(j_1,j_2)$, corresponding to the spins of the two $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebras in the decomposition $\mathfrak{so}(1,3)\simeq \mathfrak{su}(2)\oplus \mathfrak{su}(2)$, and are not unitary.

On the other hand, in QFT we are interested in representations of the Poincaré group on \emph{fields}, i.e. on vector spaces of functions of spacetime, which are infinite-dimensional and can be unitary.
We have essentially two kind of representations.
\begin{itemize}
    \item[] \textbf{Bosonic representations.} These are tensor products of the fundamental representation of the Lorentz group, i.e. the $(\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2})$ representation. The fields transform as tensors under Lorentz transformations, and their components can be labeled by spacetime indices,
    {\small\begin{align}
        B^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_m}_{\nu_1\cdots \nu_n}(x)\mapsto \Lambda^{\mu_1}_{\rho_1}\cdots \Lambda^{\mu_m}_{\rho_m}(\Lambda^{-1})^{\sigma_1}_{\nu_1}\cdots (\Lambda^{-1})^{\sigma_n}_{\nu_n}\,\,\,B^{\rho_1\cdots\rho_m}_{\sigma_1\cdots \sigma_n}(\Lambda^{-1}x),
    \end{align}}
    Notable examples include scalar fields (spin 0), vector fields (spin 1), and tensor fields (spin 2).
    %
    \item[] \textbf{Fermionic representations.} These are tensor products of spinor representations of the Lorentz group.
    The fields transform as spinors under Lorentz transformations, and their components are be labeled by spinor indices,
    {\small\begin{align}
        \psi_{r_1\cdots r_m}^{s_1\cdots s_n}(x)\mapsto S^{s_1}_{q_1}\cdots S^{s_n}_{q_n}(S^{-1})^{p_1}_{r_1}\cdots (S^{-1})^{p_m}_{r_m}\,\,\,\psi_{p_1\cdots p_m}^{q_1\cdots q_n}(\Lambda^{-1}x)\,.
    \end{align}}
    Notable examples include Dirac spinors (spin 1/2) and Rarita-Schwinger fields (spin 3/2), and more generally composite fields of arbitrary half-integer spin.
\end{itemize}
\end{mytheorem}


\begin{mytheorem}[Spontaneous symmetry breaking \& anomalies.]
In a nutshell, spontaneous symmetry breaking occurs when the underlying laws (Lagrangian or Hamiltonian) of a system possess a certain symmetry, but the ground state (vacuum state) of the system does not exhibit that symmetry.
This phenomenon has profound implications in various areas of physics, including condensed matter physics and particle physics.

Anomalies, on the other hand, refer to situations where a symmetry that is present at the classical level of a theory is broken already when the theory is quantized, i.e. it is not even a symmetry of the quantized lagrangian/hamiltonian.
Anomalies can have significant consequences, such as the violation of conservation laws associated with the broken symmetry or the non-renormalizability of the theory, and lead to inconsistencies in the theory if not properly accounted for.
Indeed, avoiding anomalies is often a guiding principle to extend quantum field theories, such as BSM extentions of the Standard Model of particle physics.
\end{mytheorem}


\begin{mytheorem}[Why the vacuum energy must be elevated: it is not empty/not a single point!]
%
In a sense, it is just natural that the energy of the vacuum is elevated when passing from classical to quantum physics, since the vacuum is not empty but rather a sea of quantum fluctuations.
This is immediately understood in the simplest case of a quantum harmonic oscillator $H=\frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 q^2$, where the ground state energy is shifted from the classical value of zero to the quantum value of $\hbar\omega/2$.
In the classical case the ground state is a single point in phase space $(q,p)=(0,0)$, while in the quantum case the ground state is a Gaussian wave packet centered at $(q,p)=(0,0)$ with non-zero width $\Delta q \Delta p = \hbar/2$.
For example, in position-space representation the ground state wave function is indeed
\begin{equation}
    \psi_0(q) = \left(\tfrac{m\omega}{\pi\hbar}\right)^{1/4} e^{-\frac{m\omega}{2\hbar}q^2}\,.
\end{equation}
The minimum of the potential is at $q=0$, but the wave function is non-zero for all $q\in\mathbb{R}$, thus exploring areas where the potential is elevated and thus increasing the energy of the ground state.
This is a general feature of quantum systems, where the uncertainty principle prevents the system from being localized in a single point in phase space, leading to a non-zero ground state energy.
\end{mytheorem}


\begin{mytheorem}[The Casimir effect. \todoinline{TBC}]
%
The Casimir effect is a physical force which arises e.g. from the zero-point energy of the \emph{quantized} electromagnetic field in the presence of conducting boundaries and pushes them closer together.
It arises since the zero-point energy of vacuum is reduced as the plates get closer thus diminishing the allowed modes of the electromagnetic field between them.
There is an extermely close analogy with the attractive force exerted between two ships travelling parallel to each other in the sea.
Indeed, waves with arbitrary wavelength can exist outside the ships and hit the rightmost ship from the right (resp. the leftmost one from the left) pushing them closer together.
On the other hand, only waves with countable discrete wavelengths $\sim L/n$ can fit between the ships and push them further apart, where $L$ is the distance between them.
There is thus a net attractive force between the ships since the pressure from outside is larger than the one from inside.

\begin{wrapfigure}[11]{r}{0.45\textwidth} % 16 = lines to wrap; adjust as needed
    \centering
    \vspace{-0.5\baselineskip}            % optional: nudge up
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{images/casimir_effect.jpeg}
    \caption{Illustration of the Casimir effect between two parallel conducting plates.}
    \label{fig:casimir_effect}
\end{wrapfigure}
In the context of QFT, consider the electromagnetic field confined between two perfectly-conducting parallel plates separated by a distance $a$.
Boundary conditions discretize the momentum perpendicular to the plates,
\begin{align}
    k_z = \frac{n\pi}{a},\quad n\in\Z\,,\qquad
    \omega_{n,\mathbf{k}_\perp} = c\sqrt{|\mathbf{k}_\perp|^2 + \Big(\tfrac{n\pi}{a}\Big)^2}.
\end{align}
The zero-point energy per unit area is formally divergent and given by
\begin{equation}
    \frac{E_0(a)}{A} = \sum_{n\in\Z}\int \frac{d^2 k_\perp}{(2\pi)^2}\, \tfrac{1}{2}\hbar\omega_{n,\mathbf{k}_\perp},
\end{equation}
The physically meaningful quantity is the \emph{difference} with empty space (no plates)
\begin{equation}
    \frac{E_{\text{Cas}}(a)}{A} := \frac{E_0(a)}{A} - \frac{E_0(\infty)}{A}.
\end{equation}
Using analytic regularization (e.g. zeta-function with $\zeta(-3)=\tfrac{1}{120}$), one obtains a finite energy per unit area, yielding an attractive force between the plates
{\small
\begin{equation}
    \frac{E_{\text{Cas}}(a)}{A} = -\,\frac{\pi^2\,\hbar c}{720\,a^3} \quad \Rightarrow\quad 
     \frac{F}{A} = -\,\frac{d}{da}\!\left(\frac{E_{\text{Cas}}}{A}\right) = -\,\frac{\pi^2\,\hbar c}{240\,a^4} < 0.
\end{equation}}
%
This force has been measured with high precision and exemplifies how vacuum fluctuations plus boundary conditions produce macroscopic effects.
Crucially, it is a direct proof that vacuum fluctuations do exist at least down to length scales of order $a$, typically microns or less in experiments, placing a lower bound on the UV cutoff of Qunatum Field Theories.
In particular, including zero-point energies only down to the micron scale, much bigger than the Planck scale, already yields a vacuum energy density $\sim 10^{60}$ times larger than the observed cosmological constant, highlighting the severity of the cosmological constant problem.
%
\end{mytheorem}
